Introduction

The American workplace is governed by a dense web of federal and state laws that are, on paper, strongly protective of workers. The Fair Labor Standards Act. Title VII. The FMLA. The ADA. OSHA. State-level equivalents that often go even further.

In practice, the vast majority of workers who experience violations never take action — not because they lack rights, but because they don't know what those rights are, and they can't afford to find out. Employers know this. And some rely on it.

The Employment Violations That Happen Every Day

Wage theft — including unpaid overtime, misclassified employees, and illegal deductions — is the single largest form of theft in the United States, affecting millions of workers annually.

Wrongful termination based on race, gender, religion, national origin, disability, and age is illegal under federal and most state laws. Workplace harassment that creates a hostile environment carries substantial legal liability.

  • ⚖️Wage theft — Unpaid overtime, misclassification, illegal deductions. The #1 form of theft in the U.S.
  • 🚫Wrongful termination — Firing based on race, gender, religion, national origin, disability, or age.
  • ⚠️Hostile work environment — Harassment severe or pervasive enough to affect working conditions.
  • 📋Retaliation — Punishing employees for reporting violations, filing complaints, or taking FMLA leave.

Yet most victims never pursue these claims. The #1 reason: they assume they can't afford a lawyer.

The Deadlines You Don't Know About — That Will Kill Your Case

Employment law is governed by strict statutes of limitations. For federal discrimination claims under Title VII, you typically have 180 to 300 days from the discriminatory act to file with the EEOC — before you can ever sue. Miss that window and your claim is permanently barred.

⏰ Critical Deadline Warning

Under Title VII, you may have as few as 180 days from the date of discrimination to file with the EEOC. After that window closes, your claim is permanently barred — no matter how strong your case.

Non-Disclosure Agreements and Severance: Read Before You Sign

One of the most common ways employers extinguish legal liability is through severance agreements and NDAs presented under time pressure. "Sign within 21 days or the offer expires" is a common tactic.

These agreements can permanently waive your right to sue for discrimination, harassment, or wage violations. Once signed, you may have no recourse — regardless of what your employer did.

A CLA attorney can review these documents immediately, explain what you are waiving, and advise whether the severance offered reflects the legal exposure the employer is trying to contain.

Gathering Evidence: What You Need to Protect Yourself

  • 📄All written communications — emails, texts, performance reviews, disciplinary notices
  • 💰Pay stubs and timekeeping records showing hours worked vs. hours paid
  • 🗓️A dated journal of verbal incidents — what was said and who witnessed it
  • 📋Your original employment contract, offer letter, and any NDAs or non-competes

Store copies outside company systems — on personal email or cloud storage — before any separation from employment occurs.
