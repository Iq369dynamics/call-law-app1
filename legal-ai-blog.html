Imagine a technology revolution that promises to transform an entire industry — and then proceeds to make things worse for the very people who need it most. That is precisely what is happening inside the American legal system right now, and almost nobody is talking about it.

In 2025, venture capitalists, sovereign wealth funds, and the world's most sophisticated investors collectively poured more than $2.4 billion into legal artificial intelligence — the highest annual total ever recorded, according to Crunchbase. On the surface, that sounds like progress. In reality, it is a story of staggering inequality dressed up as innovation.

Every single dollar of that capital has flowed toward one destination: making already-profitable law firms even more profitable.

Where the Money Went

A Billion-Dollar Arms Race for the Already Rich

Harvey, a San Francisco-based legal AI startup, raised two $300 million rounds in 2025, pushing its total funding to over $800 million. The company now serves eight of the ten highest-grossing law firms in the United States. Read that again. Eight of the ten richest law firms in America — firms that collectively bill hundreds of millions of dollars annually — are the primary beneficiaries of Harvey's AI-powered revolution.

In early 2026, Stockholm-based Legora announced a $550 million funding round led by Accel, with participation from Benchmark, Bessemer Venture Partners, General Catalyst, Iconiq, Redpoint Ventures, and Y Combinator — valuing the company at $5.55 billion, a figure that tripled in just five months.

GC AI, which makes AI tools for in-house corporate legal teams focused on drafting documents, research, and analyzing contracts, raised $60 million in a single funding round. Seattle-based Supio brought in another $60 million in Series B funding to scale its AI-powered platform specifically for personal injury and mass tort law firms.

The Cost of Justice

$1,000 an Hour — and AI Is Making Lawyers Richer, Not Cheaper

Here is the brutal arithmetic of the American legal system in 2026:

The average Am Law 100 lawyer's standard billing rate cracked the $1,000-per-hour barrier in 2025. Among all U.S. attorneys, the average hovers around $600 per hour — and profits per lawyer at the country's largest firms have surged 53.7% since 2019.

Most lawyers charge hourly rates ranging from $250 to $500, which is generally unaffordable for most people. Lawyers often require substantial upfront retainer payments before they will even begin work — and those hourly fees can compound with terrifying speed.

Some seemingly simple legal cases, like civil disputes involving car accidents, can cost as little as $10,000. More complicated cases routinely exceed $100,000. And those numbers are before court fees, expert witnesses, and filing costs — which, according to the Brennan Center for Justice, have spiked in nearly every U.S. state since 2008.

Now ask yourself: does pouring $2.4 billion into AI tools that help law firms process more documents, win more cases, and maximize billable hours bring those prices down — or push them further out of reach?

The Access to Justice Crisis — By the Numbers

Research consistently shows that the overwhelming majority of Americans with civil legal needs — evictions, debt collection, family law, employment disputes — either go unrepresented or simply abandon their legal rights entirely. The problem is not awareness. It is cost. And the $2.4 billion flowing into legal AI in 2025 did nothing to change that equation.

The Billable Hour Problem

An Industry Designed to Overcharge — and AI Is Supercharging It

Over the past 50 years, American society has gone from a time when the average middle-class person or small business could generally find affordable legal help to a time today where everyday people struggle to afford legal assistance — and lawyers often joke that they could not afford their own services if the need arose.

The engine of this dysfunction is the billable hour. The billable hour focuses on the lawyer's inputs rather than the value delivered, rewarding inefficiency rather than solutions. It also creates a profound perception of unaffordability when the client does not know what they will owe until it is too late — a dynamic that has worsened dramatically since the billing model became prevalent.

Now, AI is being used to make lawyers faster at their work — but not one major investor is requiring that efficiency savings be passed along to clients. According to Thomson Reuters, AI could save lawyers four hours per week — worth more than $100,000 in annual billable time gains per attorney. Those gains are going directly into partner profits. Not into lower bills for the person facing a landlord trying to illegally evict them.

Law firm technology spending grew 9.7% in 2025 — the fastest real growth ever experienced in the legal industry. Knowledge management tools saw even steeper growth at 10.5%, as firms bet heavily on AI-enhanced workflows to drive competitive advantage. The word "competitive" is doing heavy lifting here. The competition is not to serve more people. It is to bill more efficiently.
